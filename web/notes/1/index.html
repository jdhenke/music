The Science of Sound

Preface

This page explains how the physical mechanics of sound work, how to mathematically describe important attributes of sound, and how our ears actually perceive those attributes. Understanding the connection between the mathematical concepts and the corresponding aspects of sound will be helpful because the mathematical concepts will be used to explain and manipulate music throughout this site. This page mentions but is not exclusive to musical notes, but instead describes sound in general, leaving the specifics of musical notes, including a more precise mathematical definition of waves, for the next page. After reading this page, you should have an understanding of what makes sounds soft or loud as well as low or high pitched.

Pressure Is The Force Against A Surface

One example is pushing against a wall, your hand is applying a force against the parts of the wall it is touching, so that part of the wall is under pressure.

Another example that is closer to how hearing works is the pressure in a tire. The air inside the tire is pressing against the inner surface of the rubber of the tire, creating pressure.

In the same way, air in the atmosphere is actually applying pressure to your eardrum.

However, there is also air on the other side of the ear drum that is applying equal pressure, so your eardrum is not constantly bulging one way or the other.

However, if the pressure from the outside of your ears increases, such as when scuba diving, or decreases, such as flying in an airplane, your ears may equalize and you may feel a “pop”, which is letting air in or out from behind your eardrum to equalize the pressure so that the sensation of pressing against your eardrums goes away.

Pressure Waves Create Moving Fluctuations In Pressure

Pressure Waves are fluctuations in pressure that move through a medium.

For example, holding a slinky with both hands and shaking one side will produce a movement through the slinky where the coils get closer and farther apart.

In the same way, pressure waves can move through a gas or liquid, where the molecules get closer and farther apart.

Pressure waves move at a constant speed which depends on what the wave is moving through.

The speed of pressure waves moving through the air is around 767mph, whereas seismic waves through the earth can travel at more than ten times that speed.

Periodic Waves Repeat At Some Frequency and Have an Amplitude

Periodic waves are waves that repeat the same pattern over and over again.

How long it takes to repeat themselves is their period, or inversely, how often they repeat themselves in a given amount of time is their frequency.

For a periodic pressure wave, the maximum amount the pressure changes during each period is its amplitude.


If x(t) describes the pressure over time as a periodic pressure wave comes through, then A describes the amplitude and T describes the period, making 1/T the frequency.

Sounds Are Periodic Pressure Waves In The Air

Finally, sound itself can be described using the terminology that’s been developed so far, specifically, as periodic pressure waves that travel through the air.

This means that the source of the sound must be something that is repeatedly moving with some frequency, and displacing the air with some amplitude.

This could be the vibrating strings on a piano when it’s being played or your vibrating vocal chords when you are talking.

Your Ears Perceive Amplitude as Loudness

The amplitude of the sound wave is perceived by your ears as its loudness.

Sources that displace the air a larger amount create a louder sound.

For example, if you gently pluck a guitar string so it only moves a small physical distance, the sound is quieter. Conversely, if you pluck it hard, the string moves a larger physical distance and the sound is louder.

Most humans can hear sounds between 0 and 120-130 decibels (dB) and has to do with the ratio of the amplitude (how much the pressure changes during the wave) relative to the baseline pressure. Hearing damage can start around 90dB.

Your Ears Perceive Frequency As Pitch

The frequency of the sound wave is perceived by your ears as its pitch. Sources that create a higher frequency sound high pitched, and sources with a lower frequency sound low pitched.

For example, rumbling thunder has a low frequency while birds chirping have a high frequency.

Most humans can hear frequencies between 20Hz and 20,000Hz. Hz means per second, so a periodic wave with a frequency of 1000Hz means the wave repeats itself 1000 times per second, or once every millisecond.

Brain Teasers

If you were in a room with constant pressure twice that of sea level, could you “hear” the higher pressure?

No! If the pressure is not repeatedly changing, there’s no frequency. If you haven’t equalized your ear, you might feel a physical pressure in your ear, but the increased pressure is not itself a sound.

Why do car sirens sound higher pitched coming towards you and lower pitched going away from you?

The Doppler Effect describes this phenomenon occurring because, as the siren moves toward you, each successive wave starts a little bit closer to you, making the waves closer together, so when they get to you the wave is repeating with a higher frequency than if it was standing still.

Note: This does NOT happen because the waves themselves are traveling faster though. The speed of sound in a given medium is constant.

If a plane going faster than the speed of sound is coming towards you, what does it sound like?

Nothing! If it emitted a sound a mile away, the plane would reach you before the sound it created there did, so while the plane is still in front of you, it would be completely silent, always ahead of the sound it produces.

Summary

On this page, it was shown how sounds are really periodic pressure waves propagating through the air, which our eardrums interpret using two attributes of these waves. First, the amplitude of the wave, how much the wave temporarily changes the pressure, corresponds to how loud a sound is. Second, the frequency of the wave, how quickly the wave repeats itself, corresponds to a sound’s pitch.
